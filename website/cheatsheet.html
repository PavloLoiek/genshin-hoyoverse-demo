3. Demo Sequence Overview

The demo uses a single proven sequence: Document-First. Four uploads, each one closes a gap the previous one left. Four specific callers fail at Stage 3 and resolve beautifully at Stage 4 — that is the emotional arc.

Document-First (FAQ → API → Policy → Transcripts)

The one-line arc (memorise this): “Four uploads. FAQ, API, Policy, Transcripts. Each one closes a gap the previous one left. After the fourth upload, the same caller who failed at Stage 3 gets a beautiful resolution — without us writing a single line of code. That’s the Self-Learning flywheel.”

6. Per-Use-Case Talking Points

Account_Recovery

  • Business problem: Real-world account lockout is the #1 support volume driver for live-service games. A flat “UID required” policy fails the 12–18% of callers who don’t have it.
  • Stage progression: S1 knows policy in the abstract. S2 can call initiate_recovery but skips verification — anyone can reset Kaito’s password. S3 demands email OTP (good) but dead-ends on no-UID/no-email callers. S4 runs knowledge-based fallback (last 5★ + device + AR + region), platform-based fallback (PSN / Apple), with empathy throughout.
  • Edge case: Viktor Ivanov (UID 708817234), no UID + no email access.
  • Key metric: Recovery time from 24h (S3 escalation to human) → 4 min (S4 self-service via agent).
  • Customer angle: “This exact pattern applies to every gaming platform. Anyone with a large F2P base has Viktors calling every hour.”

Wish_And_Gacha_Disputes

  • Business problem: Gacha frustration is a top churn driver. Most frustrated players never call — they just quit.
  • Stage progression: S1 explains pity in the abstract. S2 pulls wish history and pity counter but leaks data (no verification). S3 reads the Pity Policy correctly — but robotically, turning frustrated callers into churners. S4 empathises, explains soft pity mechanics (75–89 dramatically boosted), reassures, enables in-app tips.
  • Edge case: Sophie Martin (UID 703321876), 82 pulls no 5★ on Furina, threatening to quit.
  • Key metric: At 82 pulls, soft pity has been active for 7 pulls — Furina is statistically 1–10 wishes away. A human agent saves the player. A policy-quoting agent loses them.
  • Customer angle: “How many subscribers does your best-retained customer touch each month? How many of those interactions are the difference between retention and churn?”

Top_Up_And_Refund

  • Business problem: Minor-account refund disputes end up in chargebacks, App Store complaints, and regulatory scrutiny. Mishandled refunds are expensive on both sides.
  • Stage progression: S1 quotes the refund policy. S2 processes refunds but doesn’t check the is_minor flag or require guardian verification. S3 applies “all sales final” consistently — denying legitimate MAPP refunds. S4 detects the minor, walks guardian verification, opens expedited ticket, offers parental-controls setup.
  • Edge case: Helen Williams (guardian) / Emma Williams (UID 705532108, minor, AR 45), $520 across 3 days.
  • Key metric: A $520 chargeback costs ~$40 in fees plus reputation. A MAPP-compliant refund via proper verification costs 0.
  • Customer angle: “Every platform regulator in the world is watching minor-account spending right now. The difference between S3 and S4 here is regulatory risk.”

Compensation_And_Bugs

  • Business problem: Standardised compensation is fair but feels mechanical for high-value players. Goodwill is where loyalty is built.
  • Stage progression: S1 knows the published comp table. S2 issues compensation without confirming incident match or UID region. S3 applies the table correctly but never escalates — Kaito got 300 Primogems flat. S4 applies the table AND escalates goodwill (+3 Fates, +60 Primogems, apology mail, grace period).
  • Edge case: Kaito Nakamura (UID 832458917, Asia), affected by INC202604161 (18-hour Apr 16–17 outage). Season Pass milestone affected.
  • Key metric: Goodwill escalation adds ~$8 of virtual value. Churn reduction from one well-handled high-value complaint: $100+ lifetime.
  • Customer angle: “Your comp table is fair. But your best humans know when to go beyond it. That’s what the transcripts teach the agent.”
